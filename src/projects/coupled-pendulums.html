Coupled Pendulums

A quick javascript simulation of a line of pendulums coupled with angular springs.

Open Interactive Simulation

This was intended to be a small project to integrate a relatively simple physics simulation with Three.js used as the graphics engine, primarily to become familiar with the Three.js library. I chose to model a simple chain of coupled pendulums as a starting point.

We can imagine coupling pendulums in a variety of ways, but I opted for a simple angular spring based form of coupling. Each pendulum is modeled as a rigid rod with a mass on the end (the bob). A pendulum is coupled to its next neighbor by an angular spring that applies a mutual torque to both pendulums in proportion to the difference in angle, with the proportional strength being controlled by the spring constant. This is simply 𝜏 = k Δθ, which we might note is the same form of equation for simple masses connected by springs in a linear chain.

\( \frac{d^2\vec x}{dt^2} \propto \Delta \vec x \)

In fact, many different physical systems share this same underlying physical principle, stated in the equation above, often where a force is proportional to the difference in position. This form of equation describes a system of coupled harmonic oscillators, like our pendulums, but also lattice vibrations in a solid, some molecular dynamics, wave propagation in a discrete medium (approximated water or sound waves), and even a rough model of electromagnetic fields. Much of the universe can be modelled by thinking in terms of springs.
